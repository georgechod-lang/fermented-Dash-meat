Overview

Dr. Gundry’s Take on Fermented Meat Cardiologist perspective

Dr. Steven Gundry, a cardiothoracic surgeon and author of The Plant Paradox, has become a vocal supporter of properly fermented meats as the “safest” and most heart‑friendly way to eat red meat. His focus is on how fermentation changes meat at a molecular level.

The Neu5Gc problem

Red meats from mammals (beef, pork, lamb) contain a sugar molecule called Neu5Gc. Humans don’t make Neu5Gc, but we incorporate it into our tissues when we eat red meat. The immune system can see Neu5Gc as “foreign” and attack it, driving chronic inflammation.

  • Neu5Gc is linked to higher risk of atherosclerosis, cancer, arthritis, and other inflammation‑driven diseases.
  • Traditional advice: just eat less red meat. Gundry’s question: can we neutralize Neu5Gc instead?

Where fermentation comes in

Lactobacillus and other fermenting bacteria can use Neu5Gc as a food source. When meat is properly cultured with these bacteria, they can dramatically reduce the Neu5Gc content before you ever eat it.

  • Classic European cured meats showed modest (~30%) Neu5Gc reduction.
  • Newer, targeted fermentation approaches (like the one Gundry endorses) can reduce Neu5Gc to near zero.

In Gundry’s view, this lets you “have your steak and eat it too” – enjoying red meat while avoiding much of the molecular trigger that makes it problematic for the heart and arteries.

Mechanisms & effects

Why Fermented Meat May Be Healthier

1. Less inflammation from Neu5Gc

When bacteria consume Neu5Gc during fermentation, the resulting meat delivers far less of this inflammatory sugar. That means:

  • Less immune attack on tissues that incorporate Neu5Gc.
  • Lower chronic inflammation burden on arteries and joints.
  • Potentially reduced risk of coronary disease and some cancers over time.

2. Probiotic & postbiotic effects

Properly fermented meats use live starter cultures (e.g., Lactobacillus). These:

  • Seed the gut with beneficial microbes (probiotics).
  • Produce helpful fermentation byproducts (postbiotics) that can support gut barrier and immunity.
  • May improve digestion and tolerance of red meat.

3. Cleaner ingredients & better sourcing

Gundry emphasizes that the fermented meats he recommends are:

  • From grass‑fed, pastured animals.
  • Raised without corn, soy, hormones, or routine antibiotics.
  • Free from phosphates, nitrites/nitrates, and artificial preservatives.

That aligns with his broader low‑lectin, anti‑inflammatory dietary framework.

4. Nutrient‑dense & metabolically friendly

High‑quality fermented meats can provide:

  • Complete protein for muscle and metabolic health.
  • Heme iron and B‑vitamins for energy and red blood cells.
  • Better fat quality (more omega‑3s and CLA in grass‑fed products).
  • Stable blood sugar impact (very low in carbohydrates).
Key idea: Fermentation doesn’t just preserve meat – it can chemically transform it into something that behaves differently in the body than ordinary red meat or typical processed meats.
Specific product

“Beef with Benefits” – Gundry’s Favorite Fermented Meat

When asked what he personally eats and recommends, Dr. Gundry frequently points to a line of fermented meats developed with chef Jimmy Schmidt, sold as “Beef with Benefits” by J&R Ranch Foods in the U.S.

What makes it different?

  • Source: Grass‑fed, grass‑finished Angus beef and heritage pork from pasture‑based farms (no corn or soy feed).
  • Fermentation: Meats are inoculated with probiotic cultures that aggressively reduce Neu5Gc and other undesirable compounds.
  • Low‑temperature cooking: After fermentation, meats are cooked slowly at low heat to preserve nutrients while ensuring safety.
  • Clean label: No hormones, no antibiotics, no gluten, no chemical preservatives – and the meat is effectively lectin‑free because the animals weren’t grain‑fed.

Gundry has publicly said he has no financial stake in the company and promotes these products purely because they fit his ideal of “food you love that loves you back.”

Side‑by‑side

Fermented Meat vs. Jerky & Conventional Processed Meats

Fermented Meat

  • Uses live bacterial cultures to lower Neu5Gc and pH.
  • Often from grass‑fed/pastured animals.
  • Can provide probiotic and postbiotic benefits.
  • Typically lower in additives and chemical preservatives.
  • Air‑dried/low‑temp cured → fewer heat‑derived toxins.

Jerky (non‑fermented)

  • Dehydrated meat, usually not fermented.
  • Retains full Neu5Gc content of red meat.
  • Often contains sugar, flavor enhancers, and nitrites.
  • High heat can create oxidized fats and AGEs.
  • Still better than candy, but not Gundry’s ideal.

Conventional Processed Meats

  • Bacon, ham, many deli meats, hot dogs.
  • Commonly cured with sodium nitrite and other additives.
  • Linked to higher cancer and cardiovascular risk in large studies.
  • Usually from grain‑fed, confined animals.
  • Ultra‑convenient, but highest “health cost.”
Bottom line: Fermented, pasture‑raised meats move red meat closer to a traditional whole food – while many modern processed meats move it toward an ultra‑processed product.
Takeaway

Putting It All Together

From a cardiologist‑scientist perspective, fermented meats are a compelling way to enjoy red meat while minimizing its downsides.

  • Fermentation can dramatically reduce Neu5Gc, lowering one suspected root cause of meat‑related inflammation.
  • Properly fermented meats introduce or preserve beneficial microbes and byproducts that support the gut.
  • Choosing grass‑fed, pastured, additive‑free products amplifies the benefits (better fats, fewer chemicals).
  • Compared with typical jerky and processed meats, these products have a far more favorable profile for heart health and metabolism.

In practice, this means that someone in mid‑life or older who wants good protein, strong muscles, and a healthy heart doesn’t necessarily have to give up red meat – but how that meat is raised and prepared matters. Fermented, grass‑fed options offer a way to enjoy the flavor and satiety of meat in a form that better supports long‑term health.
